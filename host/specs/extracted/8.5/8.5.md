## Introduction (page 1)

Bearing Wear Monitoring (BWM) is a method of monitoring the condition of the three major bearings of a two stroke crosshead engine. These are main-, crank pin-, and crosshead bearings. The method is based on the fact that any change in bearing wall thickness in the loaded part of one of these bearings (due to wear or seizure) will result in a corresponding change of Bottom Dead Centre (BDC) level of one or more of the crossheads relative to the engine structure. In case of crank pin- or crosshead bearing wear, the BDC level of the cylinder in question will change, while in case of main bearing wear, the BDC level of the two cylinders adjacent to this bearing will change.

The primary aim of BWM is to detect a bearing failure before it develops to an extent where heat is causing damage to other parts than the bearing shell. These could be the crosshead pin, shaft journal, or distortion of the bearing housing. Such damage will generally be the result if the bearing lining (white metal or tin-aluminium) is worn through and contact between the shaft and the bearing shell steel backing occurs.

There are two different methods to analyze the BDC level signals. One method with sudden changes in the BDC level and the other method is analysing if there are any slow wear in the bearings, which both are described in this document.

---

## Scope (page 2)

This document specifies the signal processing to be performed in order to obtain values that can be compared to operational limits such as alarm limit or slow down limit. It is assumed that a true signal representing the actual distance from each guide shoe (two for each cylinder) in its BDC to a sensor is available for every passage of BDC. The signal is to be measured with a repeatability of +/- 0.01 mm. The technical means of obtaining the signal is outside the scope of this document.

Maker's approval is valid based on revision no. 2 or later.

## 1 System Response (pages 5–6)

The BDC levels are recorded, both fore and aft, at every revolution of the engine by a proximity sensor, which can be attached to the guide bar and directed towards the lower edge of the guide shoe. In case of wear in the loaded part of one bearing, or bearing seizure, a comparison with the normal BDC level of the cylinder(s) in question will show abnormal values.

The signals in the system are to be measured with a repeatability of +/- 0.01 mm. The signal from the sensors will contain a certain level of noise. Apart from noise related to accuracy of the sensing, a certain variation of the actual guide shoe BDC level will be present due to minor irregularities in engine parameters like firing pressure etc.

Besides such noise, a dependency on the engine speed exists. This is owing to the fact that the inertia forces from piston, crosshead, connecting rod, and crank arm, loading the bearings downwards when the cylinder unit is in bottom position, increase when the rpm increases. Elastic deformation of the structure will result in a corresponding lowering of the BDC level.

Besides the speed influence, other factors like hull deformation from different vessel draft, engine temperature will have some influence on the BDC level and also the waves may influence the BDC level.

In this chapter, an overview of the different treatment steps is described, and in the following chapters, a more detailed description is shown together with values. To have control of the wear rate as early as possible, the first step of the learning mode consists of creating rough calibration curves for the different sensors.

The rough calibration curve is done in three steps. In the first step, the calibration curve is created by the average BDC value recorded at one engine speed and the expected change depending on engine size over the speed range. The next step is to add the average value of two new engine speeds that are run.

After that, the system will operate under learning mode in order to establish a full compensating curve.

The system consists of two different methods to achieve protection both against slow wear and rapid damage progress situations.

The sequence of processes for analysing slow ongoing wear is to be:

```
Signal acquiring   =>
Speed compensation =>
Noise removal (filtering) =>
Deviation calculations =>
Evaluation
```

(page 6)

The speed compensated values are filtered to minimize effects of noise, and then by using the filtered values, sensor and cylinder deviation is calculated. The sensor and cylinder deviation is calculated to deal with temperature influence, and to deal with differences of fore and aft sensor.

To detect failures causing rapid change of the BDC level, the system needs to have another type of analysis with faster reaction. To analyse such an irregularity change in the BDC-level, the sequence of processes is to be:

```
Signal acquiring  =>
Speed compensation  =>
Calculation of reference level and present state  =>
Calculation of combined sums  =>
Evaluation
```

Since this method is used to detect fast developing situations in the crankarm bearings, every single hit must be evaluated and compared to the present alarm limit.

For both sequences, the same speed compensation is used.

Due to the fact that the reference level (in the fast damage progress algorithms) has a slow update ratio, it is expected to have higher variations during rpm changes even if the speed compensation is used. Therefore, a dynamic alarm limit is used that increases the alarm limit when the engine is changing the speed. The increased alarm limit then decreases exponentially when the speed becomes stable.

When the various deviations and references are calculated, they are compared with the corresponding warning and alarm levels. If the magnitude of any reference value or deviation has passed any level, this has to be stored in a log file, and necessary actions have to be taken.

The system also stores data from the wear monitor algorithms in order to be able to visualize any possible ongoing wear.

## 2 Speed (RPM) Compensation (page 7)

The engine speed has the most significant influence on the BDC level. A variation of over 0.3 mm can exist, depending on engine size. A compensation of this influence is, therefore, necessary. Since the influence is individual for each installation, depending on layout and alignment of shaft line etc., the compensation must, for each sensor, be established individually for each installation.

The speed compensation is to be made based on a look-up table containing the normal signal values depending on engine rpm (reference values). Since this normal value may change significantly within small changes in engine rpm, the look-up table must have a fineness corresponding to dividing the speed range between 20% and 110% of nominal engine speed into no less than 100 sub-ranges, in the following designated "speed points".

The range below 20% speed is considered irrelevant for BWM. Likewise, astern running and running within the torsional vibration defined barred speed range is not to be covered.

The look-up table of engine speed compensation must be acquired during a "learning process" performed by the BWM system itself. Obviously, the learning process will take some time. So, during the first 500 h, the system is in learning mode, where the system establishes a full speed compensating look-up table for each sensor.

In case of a BWM system fitted to a new-built engine, running ship test or performing sea trial, it is, however, important that the system can offer protection of the bearings from the earliest possible time. Therefore, a rough calibration curve will be established, as soon as a 10 minute average value from one fixed engine speed is achieved. This curve is then re-adjusted so three fixed engine speeds are used to start with during the learning process.

As soon as the first speed point has been achieved the system can provide protection against rapid wear.

To get as much protection as possible it is important that the learning procedure described in this document is followed.

### 2.1 Learning Mode (page 8)

During the learning mode, the system initially uses a rough calibration curve based on 3 fixed speeds for 10 minutes at each speed. The fixed speeds have to be chosen with the following rules:

1. 20-50% of nominal speed
2. 50-80% of nominal speed
3. 80-100% of nominal speed

The 3 speeds are to be spaced by minimum 20% of nominal speed. For the three points, fixed speed is defined as fixed engine speed ± 2 rpm. The sampling is to be done after 5 revolutions have been run at the set speed.

During each period, signals of speed and sensor values (distance) are sampled and average speed and average sensor values are calculated for each speed.

After recording data for 9.5 minutes of the first speed (20-50%), the first rough calibration curve (see figure 2.1) for each sensor is generated, based on this point and a slope corresponding to a certain change in sensor distance between 20% and 110% engine speed. See chapter 7.

> **Figure 2.1, Preliminary rough calibration curve.**

After of the first compensation curves are created the system is run with the new compensation for 30 sec before increasing speed, due to establishing necessary rapid wear values. See chapter 4.

This is a preliminary curve serving as reference until the second speed has been covered. Then a new calibration curve is generated between 20% and 110% engine speed, based on the slope between the two covered speed points.

(page 9)

For the second point also 9.5 minutes of recording data is used. Similar to the first point the system is to run another 30 seconds of before increasing to the third speed.

When sampling at the 3 speeds has been completed, the rough calibration curves for each sensor are built from these 3 points by interpolation and extrapolation to cover the speed range 20-110%. See figure. 2.2.

> **Figure 2.2, Rough calibration curve.**

With the rough calibration curves established, the system will proceed into the next step of the learning mode. At this stage, the engine is operated freely. In this mode, the system is sampling sensor values in relation to the, say, 100 speed points. Once 1000 samples, corresponding to 1000 engine revolutions, have been acquired within one speed point, an average is calculated, defining the reference value, and compensation at this speed point is declared valid.

Each time a new valid point has been achieved, the speed compensation curves are to be updated by interpolate between the valid points. See figure 2.3.

> **Figure 2.3, Updated calibration curve.**

(page 10)

The three points established for the rough calibration curves are to be seen as valid until a new speed point has been achieved ± 2 rpm of the original speed point. See figure 2.4.

> **Figure 2.4, Updated calibration curve with updated initial speed point.**

Speed points for higher (or lower) speeds than the highest (lowest) valid speed point are extrapolated using the slopes from the rough calibration (slope 1 and slope 2).

The sampling of reference values is, however, to continue for totally 500 hours of engine operation. In this period, the average at each valid speed point is re-calculated every 50 hours based on all available samples apart from the original fixed 3 points, and the look-up table is updated accordingly. If the reference value at any speed point upon an update is changed by 0.2 mm or more when compared to the first obtained valid value (based on 1000 samples), an alarm has to be released, and the system must indicate this situation.

Each time a speed compensation update is done, either 1000 samples for one speed point or 50 hours of running, the rapid wear reference values are to be reset. See chapter 4.

### 2.2 Full Compensation Mode (page 11)

After the sampling during 500 hours of engine operation, reference values at some speed points may not be established due to lack of samples (less than 1000) taken at this speed. Such reference points are now defined based on the available samples, if any. In case of speed points where no samples have been taken, the reference points are defined by interpolating between nearest points with defined reference points, or - if necessary - by extrapolating according to the slopes calculated according to calculation of slopes during learning period during learning mode. See figure 2.5.

> **Figure 2.5, Final calibration curve.**

Further processing of the signal is based on the compensated sensor values, i.e. sensor signal minus reference value. One compensated value per sensor will be available at each revolution of the engine.

### 3.1 Noise Removal (page 15)

In order to minimize the influence of noise, a kind of low pass filtering is requested. A simple filter, established as a moving average which updates every time a new sensor value is received, that is at every engine revolution, can be applied. The "update ratio" is to be 5%. This means that the filtered value is to be calculated according to:

```
New_Filtered = old_Filtered * (1 - x) + new_Value * x
```

— where x is set to 0.05. Lower values of x will result in too slow reaction of the filtered signal in relation to any actual event, whereas values of x higher than 0.05 will increase the influence of noise unnecessarily.

### 3.2 Deviation Calculations (page 15)

Certain factors may influence the sensor signals in common way. As an example, a general change in engine temperature will cause all the signals to change by an almost identical value. On the other hand, change in BDC of a single cylinder will only affect the sensors related to that bearing. In order to exclude the common change of BDC level, a calculation of difference between the individual sensor signal and the average of the remaining signals is to be performed. As an example, if 8 sensor signals (s1 - s8, corresponding to a 4 cylinder engine) are available, the deviation of sensor no. 5 is calculated as:

```
d(s5) = s5 - (s1 + s2 + s3 + s4 + s6 + s7 + s8) / 7
```

— and designated **sensor deviation**.

Experience has shown that sensors on fore and aft side of the same cylinder have a tendency to produce variations in counter phase. When one signal increases, the other decreases. Such variation may be caused by variation in thrust causing a longitudinal shear formed deformation of the engine structure, or may be induced by hull deformation. It can be eliminated by calculating the average of the two signals originating from one cylinder. Using the above example of a 4 cylinder engine, the deviation of cylinder 3 (where sensors 5 and 6 are mounted) is calculated as:

```
d(c3) = (s5 + s6)/2 - (s1 + s2 + s3 + s4 + s7 + s8)/6
```

— and designated **cylinder deviation**.

By calculating the cylinder deviation, the signal scatter is reduced compared to sensor deviation. In hand of these data, cases of bearing damage have been seen where only one of the sensors in each row halve the sensitivity. Calculation of both sensor deviation and cylinder deviation is, therefore, requested.

### 3.3 Evaluation of Data (page 16)

Besides failure of a single bearing, a possibility exists that several or all bearings deteriorate in a common erosive process. This may result from oil supply failure or from pollution of the system oil. Since calculation of sensor deviation or cylinder deviation will mask such a failure, the rpm compensated sensor value itself must also be considered.

The filtered sensor values and calculated sensor- and cylinder deviations are to be compared with the corresponding levels for alarm, slow down and pre-warning. The evaluation of acquired data has to be done at least every 30 revolution.

The system is to report a "Pre-warning" status of a cylinder unit if the six-hour average "compensated sensor value" of one (or both) of the sensors in this cylinder changes by more than the Pre-warning level. See Chapter 8. The reference is the obtained values from the learning process.

The pre-warning is to indicate that the condition of any monitored bearings has started to change. If a pre-warning occurs, no immediate actions have to be taken, as long as an alarm or slow down limit has not been reached. By acknowledge the pre-warning, the pre-warning limit for the actual sensor is to be increased by 0.25mm. Any pre-warning and any reset of the pre-warning reference is to be reported to the log file (see chapter 7).

Besides the pre-warning, also an alarm or slow down can be released. These limits lead to direct action. If an alarm is reached, any of the bearings related to the alarmed unit is suspected worn and should be inspected as soon as possible.

If a slow down limit is reached, any of the bearings related to the actual unit has to be inspected as soon as possible.

All alarm levels and limits are shown in chapter 7.

## 4 Rapid Wear Algorithms (page 17)

Bearing failures, which may occur during operation, are not necessarily slow wear. It may also be a more rapid event that will be affected by different parameters, filter breakage for instance, which may not give the same response, as if it had been slow wear. Therefore, the BWM-system also needs to have algorithms that can detect situations where the BDC-level quickly changes from a stable level even within the alarm limits in the slow wear algorithms. This is done basically by calculate the difference between a fast floating average and a slow floating average. The Rapid Wear values for the different sensors are also combined in order to increase the rapidness to detect damages.

If rapid wear is detected, a serious situation is likely to exist, potentially causing damage to the engine structure within ten's of minutes. Automatic slowdown is therefore required.

Due to updating of the speed compensation curves will affect the Rapid Wear values there are needed to be corrections during the learning mode also for the Rapid Wear algorithms, where the Rapid Wear values are to be qualitative corrected for each update.

Values achieved within the torsional vibration defined barred speed range is not to be evaluated.

### 4.1 Rapid Wear Value Calulation [sic — typo] (page 17)

The Rapid Wear value for each sensor is calculated by using exponential moving averaging of the speed compensated values with very slow update coefficient, a reference can be created that will not react fast on sudden irregularities, and by comparing this average with a exponential moving average, also with speed compensated values, with fast update factor, values to defect irregularities can be created.

The exponential moving averages are created with:

```
Avg_New = Avg_Old * (1 - x) + Value_New * x
```

where Value_New are the speed compensated values.

#### 4.1.1 Rapid Slow (page 17)

The Rapid Slow value is used to state a present normal level and is supposed to react slowly. Therefore, the update factor x is set to 0.0001.

#### 4.1.2 Rapid Fast (page 18)

In contradiction to the Rapid Slow value, the Rapid Fast value is to react fast on changes. The update factor x is set to 0.2.

#### 4.1.3 Rapid Wear Sensor Value (page 18)

The Rapid Wear Sensor value for each sensor is defined by:

```
Rapid Wear Sensor = Rapid Fast - Rapid Slow.
```

### 4.2 Learning Mode (page 18)

During the learning mode where the speed compensation curves are updated, in case of a new valid speed point or 50 hours of running during first 500 h in service, the Rapid Wear values are to be corrected, not to lose any safety.

The flow chart during for the Rapid Wear algorithms during the learning period is seen in figure 4.1.

> **Figure 4.1, Flow for catch damages with fast progress.** Steps:
> 1. Acquire signal S_N (new BDC passage level) for each sensor
> 2. Speed compensate (One Point Curve): S_comp = S_N - S_ref
> 3. Check Change of Speed Compensation Curve: Has the speed compensation curves been changed within last 5 revolutions?
>    - Yes → Create 5 Rev Speed Compensated Average
> 4. Calculate ΔRPM: ΔRPM_n = |RPM_n,n - RPM_n-1|
> 5. Calculate Rapid Slow: S_Slow,n = S_Slow,n-1 * (1-x) + S_comp * x  (x=0.0001)
> 6. Calculate Rapid Fast: S_Fast,n = S_Fast,n-1 * (1-x) + S_comp * x  (x=0.2)
> 7. Calculate Rapid Wear Sensor Values: Rapid Wear = Rapid Fast - Rapid Slow
> 8. Calculate Rapid Wear Average: 5 rev Comp Avg
> 9. Calculate Unit Values: SensorRapid, CylRapid, TwinCylRapid
> 10. Calculate Alarm Levels: 5 rev Comp Avg - Rwl Avg
> 11. Check Status: Is any value above the individual dynamic alarm limit?
>    - Yes → Release Slowdown
>    - Store ("freeze") copy of short term storage
> 12. Reset Rapid Fast = 5 rev Comp Avg; Reset Rapid Slow

#### 4.2.1 Rapid Wear Sensor Value Reset (page 19)

Each time the compensation curves are changed the Rapid Wear values are to be corrected. This is done by calculate an average of speed compensated values from the first 5 revolutions after the change of the compensation curves. The Rapid Fast is then set to the speed compensation average:

```
Rapid Fast = 5 Rev Speed Compensated Average
```

Not to lose any ongoing wear since the last update the system is needed to calculate a fast updating floating average of the Rapid Wear values for each sensor according to:

```
Rapid Wear Avg_Avg,n = Rapid Wear Avg_Avg,n-1 * 0.85 + Rapid Wear * 0.15
```

and then set the Rapid Slow according to below.

```
Rapid Slow = 5 Rev Speed Compensated Average – Rapid Wear Avg
```

#### 4.2.2 Second and Third Point Reset (page 19)

Due to the compensation curves may change relatively much during first 2 changes of the speed compensation 2 extra resets are needed when the engine has reached the second and third speed.

After finished the first 10 minutes at the first engine speed the engine is suppose increases the speed to the second speed range 50-80% when a stable speed has been achieved (5 revolutions) in this range a reset is done.

The same procedure is done when second speed is finished and the third speed is achieved.

### 4.4 Combining Rapid Wear Sensor Values (page 20)

In order to increase the rapidness and be able to detect a rapid damage as soon as possible the Rapid Wear values are compared against an alarm limit both as a single value and combined together with the closest sensors as described in chapter 4.4.1 - 4.4.3.

#### 4.4.1 Single Rapid (page 20)

To get the Single Rapid value it is only to take the absolute value of the Rapid Wear value:

```
Rapid Single = abs(Rapid Wear)
```

(page 21)

The alarm for single sensor transient value is to be used as an extra safety in case the bearing is worn unevenly in axial direction.

#### 4.4.2 CylRapid (page 21)

The Cylinder Rapid value is calculated by taking the absolute value of the sum from Rapid Wear values from the sensors placed within each cylinder.

```
CylRapid = abs(Rapid Wear_FoCyl,n + Rapid Wear_AftCyl,n)
```

In figure 4.3 it is shown how to combine the sensors to calculate the CylRapid Value.

> **Figure 4.4, Flow for catch damages with fast progress.** Diagram showing "Sensors to combine for CylRapid".

The parameters to calculate the CylRapid alarm limit are possible to have a lower alarm limit than the single sensor alarm, since it has low variations. Since it is a sum of two sensors that will react in the same direction in case of wear, the reaction will be faster than a Single Rapid.

#### 4.4.3 TwinCylRapid (page 22)

For the sensors that have a close neighbour sensor in next cylinder it is also possible to combine these two sensor to create the TwinCylRapid Value.

```
TwinCylRapid = abs(Rapid Wear_AftCyl,n + Rapid Wear_FoCyl,n+1)
```

The fore most sensor, the aft most sensor and the two sensors closest to a chain drive in case of an chain drive between two cylinders are not possible to combine in this mode.

In figure 4.4 it is shown how to combine and which sensors that are possible to combine to calculate the TwinCylRapid value.

> **Figure 4.5, Sensors to use to get TwinCylRapid value.**

The TwinCylRapid value may react faster on some damages than the CylRapid value, but due to the two sensors in use for the TwinCylRapid value are not measured in two different cylinders it is expected to have higher variations and therefore needed to have a higher alarm limit not to cause false alarms.

### 4.5 Dynamic Rapid Alarm (page 22)

Even if speed compensated values are used in the calculations of the Rapid Wear values, larger variations in the different calculated values are expected when the engine speed varies.

Taking this variation into account, the alarm limits for the Rapid Wear algorithms values are dynamic and are to be increased when the engine speed is changed.

The variation of engine speed is defined by:

```
ΔRPM_n = |RPM_n - RPM_avg,n|
RPM_avg,n = RPM_avg,n-1 * (1-x) + x*RPM_n          x=0.003
```

If the deltaRPM value is larger than 4 rpm, deltaRPM is set to 4 rpm. This is to have a quick increase of the alarm limit, but not an unnecessary high alarm limit.

(page 23)

For revolutions with delta RPM higher than 15 the damage monitoring is going to pause mode and no alarms or slow down is to be given. The only value to be updated during the pause mode is deltaRPM.

The dynamic alarm limit is chosen the highest alarm level between two calculated alarm levels.

```
Alarm_Dynamic = max(Alarm_1, Alarm_2)
```

The two alarm levels are calculated with following equations:

```
Alarm_1 = Alarm_Basic * ( 1 + ΔRPM_increment_n / k )
Alarm_2 = Alarm_Basic * ( 1 + (ΔRPM_increase_at_alarm / k) * exp( -Hits/β ) )
```

ΔRPM_increase_at_alarm is the ΔRPM used in equation for Alarm_1 last time Alarm_2 was higher than Alarm_1. The parameter Hits is reset to zero every time Alarm_2 is larger than Alarm_1. For the next revolution of the engine Hits = 1 will be used in the equation for Alarm_2. All parameters for calculating the dynamic alarm level are shown in chapter 7.

### 4.6 Reporting to Ship AMS-system (page 23)

In case of a Rapid Wear released Slow Down the system is to report which Section that corresponds to the slow down as follows:

Single Rapid and CylRapid is to give a section alarm for the same section as the cylinder number the actual sensors are placed in.

TwinCylRapid is to give section alarms to the both cylinders that are used to create the TwinCylRapid Slow Down.

## 5 Sensor Adjustment (page 24)

> **Δ8.5→8.6**: In 8.6 this is renamed to "Sensor Failure" and split into 5.1 (Corrections for Failed Sensors) and 5.2 (Re-adjustment of Reference Curve, with three procedures 5.2.1/5.2.2/5.2.3 plus 5.2.4 further actions). 8.5 only has a single combined procedure.

At some time during the operation life of the system, it will probably become necessary to re-adjust the reference curve of one or several sensors. This may apply if a bracket supporting the sensor(s) has been removed due to overhaul of a main bearing. When refitting the bracket, it must be expected that the sensor(s) will locate differently.

Likewise, the replacement of a faulty sensor, or the case of a slightly bent bracket, may call for an adjustment. A procedure is therefore needed, that enables offset adjustment of individual sensors on the request of the operator.

During offset adjustment, the signals from the sensor(s) in question are to be corrected according to the existing rpm-dependant reference curves of these sensors. While normally giving values distributed around zero, the corrected value of these sensors will now reflect the physical offset of the sensors. This is to be averaged over a period of 50 hours of operation, after which the reference curves of the affected sensors are offset accordingly.

However, wear may theoretically have occurred during the time from the sensor fails until it has been replaced and offset adjusted. Such wear can be detected by the other sensor in the same cylinder (cylinder neighbour). To get the slope and offset following 3 different best fit Root Mean Square (RMS) lines based on the 6 hours average of compensated and filtered sensor values are to be done. See figure 5.1

> **Figure 5.1, Curves to be used for readjustment.**

Following assumption is to be taken:
- Last 500 hours before breakage of the broken sensor:                       a₁t + b₁
- 500 hours after replacement of a new sensor for the new sensor:            a₂t + b₂
- The cylinder neighbour sensor during the time of broken sensor:            a₃t + b₃

(page 25)

Following assumption is to be taken, during time of breakage (tb) the broken sensor has the same slope as the cylinder neighbour sensor. When 500 hours ran with a replaced sensor the offset (O) can be calculated. For the 500 h after readjustment the slope from the new sensor is used.

```
O = O₁ + a₁ * t_b + 500 * a₃
```

If both sensors for one cylinder are damaged at the same time reference is lost and this is to be noted in the log file.

During the 500 hour period, signals from the sensors under adjustment are to be considered invalid when calculating "sensor deviation" and "cylinder deviation" of other sensors/cylinders.

Likewise, alarm or slow down is not to be released due to sensor value of these sensors. After the 500 hours, the sensor values, corrected by the offset reference curves and the wear offset from the cylinder neighbour, are considered valid and the system resumes its normal routines.

In case of sensors are adjusted the Rapid Wear sensor value for the adjusted sensor is reset according to:

```
Rapid Fast = 5 Rev Speed Compensated Average
Rapid Slow = 5 Rev Speed Compensated Average – Rapid Wear Avg
```

> **★ Δ8.5→8.6→8.7**: This Rapid Slow formula evolved across all three revisions:
> - **8.5**: `Rapid Slow = 5 Rev Speed Compensated Average – Rapid Wear Avg` (uses fast-updating Avg)
> - **8.6**: `Rapid Slow = 5 Rev Speed Compensated Average – Rapid Wear (before reset)` (uses Rapid Wear value, not Avg)
> - **8.7**: `Rapid Slow = 0` (literal zero, much simpler)

## 6 Storage and Presentation of Data (page 26)

Data storage in the BWM system serves two purposes. One is to be able to retrieve the data, should any bearing damage have taken place. This can be considered as a "black box" function. The second purpose relates to the inspection of the bearings. Traditionally, bearings are opened up for routine inspections on a time-based schedule. E.g. every bearing must be opened at, say, 4-year intervals.

To avoid unnecessary opening of the bearings, it is desired to shift from time-based inspections to condition-based inspections. For this purpose, trend curves showing if any wear has taken place during the inspection interval, are to be presented to the class surveyor.

The BWM system must facilitate storage for each sensor of the following data.

- **Short term storage** is to contain the filtered values, with time stamp, available from latest 24 hours. A minimum density of one set of data for every 30 engine revolutions is required. In case an alarm limit is exceeded, a copy of the from short term storage is to be taken and stored separately as a "frozen" copy. The copy is to be extended by 5 minutes after the time of the alarm.

- **Rapid Wear sensor values floating storage** of last 2 hour of running that can be downloaded in case of an alarm or slowdown extended 5 minutes in case of an slow wear alarm or until the engine is stopped in case of a slowdown.

- **Long term storage** is to contain maximum, minimum, and average filtered values, for every 6 running hours, with time stamp. This storage must extend 5 years back.

A log has to contain the following information:

1. Any alarms, slowdowns or pre-warnings released or changes during the learning process of the BWM system.
2. Possible replacement and/or offset adjustment of any sensors.
3. Change of sensor reference from rough calibration to fine calibration
4. Possible reset of reference level for pre-warning

All information has to be time stamped and all storage and event logs are to be kept in a non-volatile memory.

Downloading of stored data and event log to an external device like a PC must be possible for the purpose of presentation to surveyors from classification societies. For this purpose one XML file has to be made, including the four parts seen below. Their format is defined in appendix A-D.

(page 27)

In the file, the sensors are to be named by cylinder number (1=for) and then 1 (fore) or 2 (aft) as an example 7.1 and 7.2 for cylinder 7.

- **Engine info part**. This is to contain information of the ship and engine. See Appendix A
- **Log part** of all changes in system status and sensor status according to the event log. All the changes are to be presented with time stamp. See Appendix B.
- **Filtered value trend part**. Derived from "Long time storage". Following data to be presented for each sensor every six engine operating hours: Time stamp, Engine operating hours, and 6 h average filtered value. See Appendix C.
- **Status part**. The purpose of this file is to provide a quick overview of each cylinder showing if opening of the bearings related to this cylinder for inspection is justified due to detected wear or due to other circumstances. These circumstances could be detection of wear exceeding the "significance limit" during exchange of a sensor or loss of reference (see section 4). The status is to be shown as a four level definition. The four levels are to be:
  - Normal "N" indicating that no wear has been detected beyond the pre-warning limit.
  - Pre-Warning "W" indicating that wear has been detected beyond the pre-warning limit.
  - Alarm "A" indicating that an alarm has been released from sensors connected to this cylinder.
  - Unknown "U" indicating that a sensor of this cylinder has lost reference or its reference curve has been corrected due to wear detected by its "neighbour" during exchange. The same indication is to be given if a damaged sensor remains unchanged.

Each part of the XML-file has to have the possibility to be generated individually and then with a time stamp when the file is generated.

Besides the unloading for class survey purpose, the following data must be possible to unload:

- The complete data of long term storage.
- Short term storage.
- Reference curves of each sensor.

No format is, however, specified for this unload.
